Statement of Purpose

          I grew up in an isolated, rural town where you had to drive an hour and a half to get to the nearest movie theatre. There were no book stores either. The school did not have a real computer or art program. I am dyslexic, and had some trouble in with reading and writing. A teacher took note and suggested my parents get me some typing and reading education games. I went from being one of the slowest readers in the class to having the top reading scores. Interactive technology and games changed my life. It gave me chances that I would not have had growing up where I did. It's why I believe so strongly in the potential of games and the interactive medium as a force for good in the world.

          I want to push the boundaries of interactive education and games. My primary professional experience is not in video games, but in the related field of UX Design. I see gaming and UX as being linked in a fundamental way, not merely through UI but in design principles. The kinds of projects that I would like to foster are the ones that blur the lines between experience and game, toy and tool, play and education. In order to build these kinds of projects, I believe that I need to learn more about business development, and hone my skills as a designer, artist, and product manager.

          I went to Brandeis University for Film and Fine Art. I originally went to Brandeis because I wanted to be an illustrator and concept artist for video games or film, but quickly realized that our art department was not commercially focused. Despite getting into the Pratt Institute as a transfer with merit scholarship, I chose to stay at Brandeis for financial reasons. I took every class, grant, and extra program I could to fill out my education, while also practicing my craft outside of class and on my own time. If I was to stay, then I was going to blaze my own trail.

          While studying abroad in London at the Slade School of Art, I came into contact with the London Indies, an indie game developer collective. I participated in the 2012 MolyJam, an international game jam based on the twitter account @PeterMolydeux, a loving homage/parody of Peter Molyneux. I did not think an oil painter and cinephile would be welcome in the industry, but I made many friends at that jam, and found a renewed drive to pursue work in the game industry despite my less than typical game education. Oh, and I also got to meet and talk with Peter!

          When I came home from London, I threw myself into my two senior thesis projects. My fine arts thesis was an experimental art installation, founded in my study of game and interactive design, and based on the founding game design text Homo Ludens (Man as Player) by Johan Huizinga.Our film department cut all our production classes my senior year. I was one credit away from getting my second degree, and that credit was supposed to be senior short film thesis. I decided to forgo that official credit and degree so that I could focus on what I really cared about, making my film. For my "unofficial" film thesis I created a student film production collective. As a result, I made the most ambitious film ever produced at our University with no involvement from the department proper. I raised my own funding, trained my own crew (because there was no longer any production classes to teach them), auditioned professional actors, negotiated contracts with music record companies for licensed music, and performed PR. While my art installation was in the realm of the theoretical, my production company experience was a crash course in practical production necessities.

          After graduation I wanted to get my hands dirty with more practical experience, so I came back to my home state and moved to Austin. I've mostly worked as a UX Designer for startups. Most relevant to this application is my work as a Usability Analyst for KingsIsle Entertainment. I originally began as a QA web tester but was switched over to analyst work within weeks for their web to mobile migration, due to my previous UX designer experience and knack for translating developer-speak into coherent and concise documentation. I was the lead analyst on the project, and worked as the assistant director between departments. I organized the stagnating JIRA backlog and got everyone up to speed and on the same page. I also participated in two company game jams as a UI artist and producer. I learned a lot, and gave me a deep dive into the game development and production process. While I wanted to work in games, I decided to leave for a UX Designer and Motion Artist position at Lodestone Social. Now I'm working a full-time UI Developer internship at Dell, helping design and develop the enterprise-level Angular.JS sales platform. I've also recently joined the non-profit HealthStart Foundation as a volunteer project manager after working on an education game with the company at the Hack4Change hackathon, one of the many hackathons I've participated in.

          I participate in at least one hackathon every other month, sometimes more if I can find them. At one recent hackathon, the 2015 Educational SXSW Game Jam, our team ran into a crisis. My co-worker and friend who was going to be our primary coder, had to leave last minute due to personal reasons. I didn't let this stop us, I stepped up to the role of coder and produced a minimal viable prototype, despite having almost no previous code experience. I see myself as fulfilling a unique role, a creative with a technical mind. I know that I am not a developer. I never went to a school for game design, and I've only worked at a single game company for 7 months, but I've learned a lot on my own time, and I think a have a lot to contribute to the program. I have a solid background in art, and my oil painting experience gives me fresh perspectives on art in games that are often overlooked.. I'm learning more code every day, and see it as a powerful tool for expression. I specifically took the UI development internship at Dell because of the opportunity it would give me to improve my code skills. This hybrid background helps me work fluently between different departments, while also keeping larger issues of strategy in mind.

          It is not enough to learn the ropes and get a steady job in the games industry. The real change doesn't just happen in making the product, it happens in making the production. I've made sure to carefully note the many different leadership and management styles at every company I've worked at. There is a lot more I need to learn about production and the business, building a network, sourcing and managing talent, raising funding, and working with publishers/investors. I've proven my leadership and drive, and handled large student film budgets and crews, but there's more I need to learn and experience before I can make the positive changes I want in the world.

          I want to see the medium evolve and achieve new heights. I want to use the medium to help people and make people's lives better. I believe that the DSGA will help me acquire the necessary tools to do that.
