Unraveling: Epithelium


          Unraveling: Epithelium was part of my senior thesis for art at Brandeis University. I knew I wanted to do interactive design as a career, but since our art department is fairly small, and doesn't have any digital art classes, I needed to figure out a way to connect my traditional fine art education, and my interest in interactive design. I used Johan Huizinga's book "Homo Ludens" as a founding text. I sought to connect concepts of modern conceptual installation art with principles of interactive and game design. I had a diverse set of influences, namely, The Stanley Parable (made here in Austin), the alt-theatre experience "Sleep No More", interactive galleries I had seen while in London, religious rituals from around the world, mythology, and the museum space.

          The original grant funding request can be found here . We received a fraction of our initial goal and had to dial back our scope a lot, but it was still a great learning experience, brining together a diverse group of artists to execute on a collaborative effort that required good logistics, and addressing technical concerns while also doing justice to the justice and intent of the original vision.

          The essential structure of the final project was a 20 foot geodesic dome, in which players/participants were invited into by an actor. Once inside the dome, they found an installation of lights and yarn sculptures. The purpose was to create a sensory rich and isolated environment. Once the player/participant decided they wanted to leave, they would speak to another actor at the exit, who would give them a choice. If they wanted to leave, they would have to make a sacrifice at the altar. They would "sacrifice" their sight, which meant being blind folded and lead out of the installation. They would then be lead into an additional installation, a large white box, which locked from the outside, and had a reverse peep-hole. They would then be instructed to take off their blind fold. Going from a low light and dim environment to a bright white and claustrophobic environment had quite an effect, and doubly so because they were locked in the box while other's peered in. After awhile we'd let them out. It was a really interesting experiment, and I've heard similar kinds of projects getting more traction here in Austin and San Francisco.

          This isn't of course game design. This isn't going to show you anything about my ability to use Unity, or model a 3D character, or animate, but I think it does a good job of showing my ambition and leadership, and the depth of thought I've given to not only game design but to interactive design as whole.
